Early in my consulting career, I thought over-delivering was always a virtue. If the client asked for feature A and I could throw in feature B for free, that was good business. It builds goodwill. It shows you care.

I was wrong.

<div class="callout">
Over-delivery does not build trust. It blurs scope. And blurred scope is where projects die.
</div>

My manager taught me this. He has a framework he applies consistently: we own the core configuration. The customer owns everything else. Placeholder components are the customer's responsibility to swap out later. This is not laziness. It is clarity.

When I started describing work that should have landed on the customer's plate, my manager would gently redirect. "The priority for now is internal verification." He was not shutting me down. He was protecting the boundary between what we were paid to do and what we were not.

The problem with over-delivering is that it sets an implicit expectation. The customer starts to believe that the extra work is part of the package. When the next project comes around, they expect the same treatment. And when you finally say no, you look like the bad guy.

I have learned to ask a different question now. Instead of "can I do more?" I ask "is this ours to do?" If the answer is no, I stop. I document it. I hand it back. It feels uncomfortable at first, especially when you have the skills to solve the problem in five minutes. But the discomfort of saying no is nothing compared to the pain of an un-scoped project that bleeds into every sprint.

Professionalism is not about doing everything. It is about doing exactly what you committed to, and nothing less. The boundary is not a wall. It is a contract. Respect it.
