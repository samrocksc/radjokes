I learned a hard lesson recently. It came not from a code review or a failed deployment, but from a single sentence I said in a meeting: "I don't know what X means."

The person on the other end was a deeply technical service owner who had already thought through the broader design of whatever we were discussing. To him, "I don't know what X means" did not sound like a request for clarification. It sounded like a challenge to his judgment.

<div class="callout">
Frame unknowns as "who can help me," never as "I don't know." The first is a path forward. The second is a closed door.
</div>

A colleague pulled me aside afterward and gave me a simple reframe. Instead of saying "I don't know," say "I will reach out to the team and find out how to integrate." Same information. Completely different signal.

This is not about pretending to know things you do not. It is about understanding that senior stakeholders are not looking for you to confess ignorance. They are looking for you to demonstrate resourcefulness. The difference between "I do not know" and "I know who to ask" is the difference between a blocker and a plan.

I have started applying this everywhere. When a client asks about a technology I have not used, I say "Let me research that and come back with options." When a teammate asks about a system I have not touched, I say "I will sync with the person who owns it."

The words change, but the honesty stays the same. The only thing that shifts is the signal you send about your ability to navigate the unknown. And in consulting, that signal is everything.
